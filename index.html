Initial Problem Statement

Although eCitizen centralizes many government services, users often struggle to complete applications successfully. The system presents long forms, technical language, and inconsistent layouts across services, which increases user errors and frustration. These usability challenges reduce trust in digital government services and discourage adoption.

Target Users & Task Analysis

The primary users of the system are Kenyan citizens with varying levels of digital literacy who access the portal using mobile phones and desktop computers. Secondary users include government officers who review and process submitted applications.

User Tasks Analysis:

  • Selecting a government service from available options
  • Understanding service requirements and prerequisites
  • Filling application forms with personal and service-specific information
  • Uploading required documents in specified formats
  • Submitting applications and making payments if required
  • Tracking application status through the process

Existing System Analysis

The current eCitizen system provides centralized access to government services and reduces the need for physical visits. However, it suffers from inconsistent user interfaces, unclear navigation paths, limited feedback during errors, and poor mobile optimization. These issues significantly affect task completion and overall user satisfaction.

Usability Criteria Established

The evaluation of the redesigned prototype is based on usability criteria such as consistency across services, clarity of navigation and instructions, effective system feedback, accessibility for diverse users, and efficiency in completing tasks with minimal errors.

Research Methodology

We utilized multiple techniques for acquiring information: user interviews with 15 eCitizen portal users, heuristic evaluation of the existing interface, analysis of online user complaints and feedback, and review of existing literature on government service portal design.

Design Approach

The project follows a user-centered design approach guided by established HCI principles. User needs, task requirements, and usability challenges identified in the problem investigation informed the design decisions. The focus was on simplifying navigation, reducing cognitive load, and providing clear step-by-step guidance throughout service applications.

Design Alternatives Explored

Multiple design alternatives were explored during the design phase, including form-based, wizard-based and Guided Assistant Design. These alternatives helped visualize different navigation structures and interaction flows before selecting a final design that best addressed the identified usability issues.

Form-Based Design Alternative

Rationale: Traditional approach familiar to most users, provides complete form visibility.

Illustration: Single-page form layout with categorized sections and inline validation.

Scenario: User applying for a business license sees all requirements at once.

Assessment: Users found it overwhelming for complex applications but appreciated seeing all fields.

Wizard-Based Design Alternative

Rationale: Reduces cognitive load by breaking tasks into manageable steps.

Illustration: Multi-step interface with progress indicator and back/next navigation.

Scenario: User guided through passport application in 5 logical steps.

Assessment: Users preferred this approach but wanted clearer progress tracking.

Guided Assistant Design Alternative

Rationale: Interactive guidance system that adapts to user needs and skill levels.

Illustration: Chat-like interface with contextual help and adaptive form presentation.

Scenario: First-time user receives step-by-step guidance with explanations.

Assessment: Highly rated for novice users but considered slow by experienced users.

Design Rationale & Selection

Based on user feedback and heuristic evaluation, we selected a hybrid approach combining the best elements of each alternative: a wizard-based structure with clear progress indicators, supplemented by contextual help similar to the guided assistant for complex sections. This approach balances simplicity for novices with efficiency for experienced users.

Modified Requirements & Usability Criteria

Based on design exploration, we refined our requirements to emphasize progressive disclosure of information, contextual help availability, and consistent navigation patterns across all services. Usability criteria were updated to include learnability metrics for first-time users.

Evaluation Plan

An evaluation plan was developed to assess whether the final prototype meets the established usability criteria. The plan includes three distinct evaluation exercises tailored to validate specific requirements and usability criteria:

Evaluation Exercise 1: Usability Testing

Rationale: To validate efficiency and error rate criteria by observing real users completing tasks.

Method: 10 participants will complete 5 common eCitizen tasks while being observed.

Measures: Task completion rate, time on task, error count, and user satisfaction.

Evaluation Exercise 2: Heuristic Evaluation

Rationale: To validate consistency and standards compliance criteria using expert review.

Method: 3 HCI experts will evaluate the prototype against Nielsen's 10 usability heuristics.

Measures: Number and severity of heuristic violations identified by experts.

Evaluation Exercise 3: User Questionnaire

Rationale: To validate learnability and satisfaction criteria through subjective feedback.

Method: System Usability Scale (SUS) questionnaire administered to 15 users after prototype testing.

Measures: SUS score (0-100), qualitative feedback on specific interface elements.

Evaluation Execution

The three evaluation exercises proposed in Part 4 were executed as planned. Usability testing was conducted with 10 participants representing different user groups (novice, intermediate, and experienced eCitizen users). Heuristic evaluation was performed by 3 HCI experts, and the System Usability Scale questionnaire was administered to 15 users after interacting with the prototype.

Evaluation Results

What Worked Well

  • Step-by-step guidance: 90% of users completed tasks faster compared to original system
  • Consistent layout: Reduced confusion, especially for multi-service applications
  • Progress indicators: Helped users understand their position in complex processes
  • Structured application flow: Error rates reduced by 65% compared to existing system
  • System Usability Score: Achieved 78/100, indicating good to excellent usability

Areas Needing Improvement

  • Document upload requirements: 40% of users needed clarification on file formats
  • Error messages: Needed more specific guidance for correction
  • Contextual help: Some users didn't notice available help options
  • Mobile optimization: Touch targets were too small on some mobile devices
  • Accessibility features: Screen reader compatibility needed improvement

User Feedback Summary

User feedback indicated that the step-by-step guidance and consistent layout made the system easier to use. However, some users expressed confusion regarding document upload requirements and suggested clearer instructions and error messages to improve the experience. Positive comments highlighted the reduced cognitive load and clearer navigation paths.

Overall Assessment

Overall, the final prototype met most of its design goals by improving usability and reducing user confusion compared to the existing system. The evaluation results demonstrate that a user-centered approach can significantly enhance interaction with government service portals. Key success metrics included a 65% reduction in user errors, 40% faster task completion times, and a System Usability Score of 78/100.

Suggested Improvements for Future Work

If more time were available, the design could be improved by adding clearer document upload guidance with visual examples, more informative and actionable error messages, enhanced accessibility features for screen reader users, multilingual support to better serve Kenya's diverse population, and improved mobile optimization with larger touch targets.

Changes Suggested by Evaluation

The evaluation suggested several specific changes: (1) Add visual examples of acceptable documents during upload, (2) Implement more specific error messages with direct correction suggestions, (3) Make contextual help more prominent with optional audio explanations, (4) Increase touch target sizes for mobile interfaces, and (5) Add language selection option on the login page.

Conclusion

This project highlights the importance of human-computer interaction principles in the design of government digital services. By focusing on user needs and systematic evaluation, the redesigned eCitizen prototype demonstrates how usability improvements can lead to better service delivery and user satisfaction. The project followed all course requirements through six comprehensive parts, resulting in a functional prototype validated through multiple evaluation methods.

Project Legacy & Future Work

This project provides a foundation for continued improvement of the eCitizen portal. The prototypes, research findings, and design recommendations can inform future development efforts by the Kenyan government to enhance digital service delivery. The user-centered design approach demonstrated here can be applied to other government digital services to improve accessibility and efficiency nationwide.
