Existing System Analysis

The current eCitizen system provides centralized access to government services and reduces the need for physical visits. However, it suffers from inconsistent user interfaces, unclear navigation paths, limited feedback during errors, and poor mobile optimization. These issues significantly affect task completion and overall user satisfaction.

Design Alternatives: Rationale & Analysis

We explored three distinct design alternatives to address the core usability issues identified in our research. Each was chosen to test a different hypothesis about how to best support users with varying digital literacy.

1. Form-Based Design

Why Chosen: This alternative was selected as a baseline, replicating the existing system's structure but with improved visual organization. It tests whether clarity can be achieved through better layout alone, without changing the fundamental interaction model.

Usability & Appropriateness: It is appropriate for experienced or task-focused users who value speed and have a clear mental model of the required information. Its usability strength is providing a complete overview, allowing users to skip around and fill known fields quickly.

Unique Element: It is the only design that presents all input fields simultaneously, which supports non-linear completion and gives users full context and control upfront. However, this is also its weakness for complex forms, as it can cause cognitive overload.

2. Wizard-Based Design

Why Chosen: This alternative was chosen to directly combat cognitive overload by decomposing long, complex applications (like business licenses or passport renewals) into a sequence of simple, manageable steps.

Usability & Appropriateness: It is highly appropriate for procedural or lengthy tasks and for novice or uncertain users. Its key usability feature is reducing errors and anxiety by presenting one logical piece of the task at a time, with clear "Next" and "Back" controls.

Unique Element: This design uniquely provides a visual progress indicator (e.g., "Step 2 of 5"), which is absent in the other two alternatives. This gives users a clear sense of location within the process and manages expectations about time and effort remaining.

3. Guided Assistant Design

Why Chosen: This alternative was developed to address the need for contextual, just-in-time help. It was chosen to test if an interactive, conversational interface could better support users who don't understand government terminology or process requirements.

Usability & Appropriateness: It is most appropriate for first-time users or those with low digital literacy. Its core usability principle is adaptive guidanceâ€”the system can ask clarifying questions and explain why information is needed, which builds user confidence and understanding.

Unique Element: The defining feature not found in the others is its conversational, question-and-answer interaction style. Unlike the static forms or linear wizard, it can branch its dialogue based on user input, potentially simplifying forms by hiding irrelevant sections dynamically.

Design Rationale & Final Selection

Based on user feedback and heuristic evaluation, we selected a hybrid approach combining the best elements of each alternative. Our final prototype uses a wizard-based structure with clear progress indicators for its manageability, supplemented by contextual help modules inspired by the guided assistant for complex sections. This balances the need for clear structure (from the wizard) with the flexibility to provide help (from the assistant), while maintaining a clean, consistent layout superior to the basic form-based approach.

Part 5: Evaluation Execution & Results

Project Website - Part 5

Facilitator observing a user during a usability testing session

Charts and graphs showing quantitative results from our evaluation

Team discussion synthesizing findings from the evaluation phase

What Worked Well

  • Step-by-step guidance improved task completion
  • Consistent layout reduced confusion
  • Error rates reduced by 65%
  • Achieved System Usability Score of 78/100

Areas Needing Improvement

  • Document upload requirements needed clarification
  • Error messages needed more specific guidance
  • Mobile touch targets were too small
