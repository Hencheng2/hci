Phase 1: Understand the Problem

Project Overview

The eCitizen portal serves as Kenya's primary digital gateway to government services, but users face significant challenges due to complex navigation, inconsistent interfaces, and unclear instructions. This project aims to redesign the portal for improved accessibility, efficiency, and user satisfaction.

Problem Statement

Kenyan citizens struggle to complete government service applications online due to complex forms, technical jargon, inconsistent layouts, and poor mobile optimization. These issues lead to high abandonment rates, increased support calls, and reduced trust in digital government services.

Phase 2: Research & Analysis

Research Methodology

We employed a mixed-methods approach to understand user needs, behaviors, and pain points when interacting with the eCitizen portal.

User Interviews

15 in-depth interviews with diverse eCitizen users

Task Analysis

Observation of users completing 5 common service applications

Heuristic Evaluation

Expert review against Nielsen's 10 usability heuristics

Device Usage Study

Analysis of mobile vs desktop usage patterns

Key Research Findings

  • Cognitive Overload: Forms with 50+ fields displayed at once overwhelm users
  • Inconsistent Design: Different layouts for similar services cause confusion
  • Poor Error Feedback: Generic messages without guidance frustrate users
  • Mobile Unfriendly: Tiny touch targets and poor responsiveness on mobile devices
  • Technical Jargon: Government terminology without explanations creates barriers
  • Unclear Navigation: Users get lost in complex menu structures
  • Document Confusion: Unclear requirements for uploads lead to errors

Target Users

The primary users are Kenyan citizens with varying digital literacy who access services via mobile phones and desktops. Secondary users include government officers who process applications. The system must support both novice and experienced users while remaining efficient for administrative processing.

Phase 3: Design Solutions

Design Principles

Our design approach was guided by these core principles derived from user research findings:

Simplicity

Reduce cognitive load through progressive disclosure and clear information hierarchy

Consistency

Uniform design patterns and interactions across all government services

Guidance

Provide clear, contextual help throughout the application process

Accessibility

Ensure all users, including those with disabilities, can access services

Design Alternatives Explored

Form-Based Design

Traditional layout with all fields visible. Appropriate for experienced users who value speed and have clear mental models.

Wizard-Based Design

Guided step-by-step process. Reduces cognitive overload by presenting one logical piece of the task at a time.

Guided Assistant

Interactive, conversational interface. Provides adaptive guidance based on user responses and understanding.

Final Design Rationale

We selected a hybrid approach combining wizard-based structure with contextual help modules. This balances clear structure for complex applications with flexible guidance where users need support, while maintaining consistency across services.

Phase 5: Evaluate & Refine

Evaluation Methodology

We conducted comprehensive usability testing to validate our design decisions and identify areas for improvement.

Usability Testing

Method: 10 participants completing 5 common tasks

Measures: Success rate, time on task, error count

Heuristic Evaluation

Method: 3 HCI experts reviewing against Nielsen's heuristics

Measures: Number and severity of violations

User Questionnaire

Method: System Usability Scale (SUS) with 15 users

Measures: SUS score (0-100), qualitative feedback

Key Findings

What Worked Well

  • Step-by-step guidance improved task completion
  • Consistent layout reduced user confusion
  • Progress indicators increased user confidence
  • Mobile optimization enhanced accessibility

Areas for Improvement

  • Document upload requirements need clarification
  • Error messages could be more specific
  • Contextual help visibility could be improved
  • Some government terms still confused users
